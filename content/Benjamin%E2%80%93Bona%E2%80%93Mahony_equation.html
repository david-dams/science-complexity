From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia

The Benjamin–Bona–Mahony equation (BBM equation, also regularized long-wave equation; RLWE) is the partial differential equation

u t + u x + u u x − u x x t = 0. {\displaystyle u_{t}+u_{x}+uu_{x}-u_{xxt}=0.\,}

This equation was studied in Benjamin, Bona, and Mahony (1972) as an improvement of the Korteweg–de Vries equation (KdV equation) for modeling long surface gravity waves of small amplitude – propagating uni-directionally in 1+1 dimensions. They show the stability and uniqueness of solutions to the BBM equation. This contrasts with the KdV equation, which is unstable in its high wavenumber components. Further, while the KdV equation has an infinite number of integrals of motion, the BBM equation only has three.[2][3]

Before, in 1966, this equation was introduced by Peregrine, in the study of undular bores.[4]

A generalized n-dimensional version is given by[5][6]

u t − ∇ 2 u t + div φ ( u ) = 0. {\displaystyle u_{t}-\nabla ^{2}u_{t}+\operatorname {div} \,\varphi (u)=0.\,}

where φ {\displaystyle \varphi } is a sufficiently smooth function from R {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} } to R n {\displaystyle \mathbb {R} ^{n}} . Avrin & Goldstein (1985) proved global existence of a solution in all dimensions.

Solitary wave solution

[edit]
See also: Cnoidal wave

The BBM equation possesses solitary wave solutions of the form:[3]

u = 3 c 2 1 − c 2 sech 2 ⁡ 1 2 ( c x − c t 1 − c 2 + δ ) , {\displaystyle u=3{\frac {c^{2}}{1-c^{2}}}\operatorname {sech} ^{2}{\frac {1}{2}}\left(cx-{\frac {ct}{1-c^{2}}}+\delta \right),}

where sech is the hyperbolic secant function and δ {\displaystyle \delta } is a phase shift (by an initial horizontal displacement). For | c | < 1 {\displaystyle |c|<1} , the solitary waves have a positive crest elevation and travel in the positive x {\displaystyle x} -direction with velocity 1 / ( 1 − c 2 ) . {\displaystyle 1/(1-c^{2}).} These solitary waves are not solitons, i.e. after interaction with other solitary waves, an oscillatory tail is generated and the solitary waves have changed.[1][3]

Hamiltonian structure

[edit]

The BBM equation has a Hamiltonian structure, as it can be written as:[7]

u t = − D δ H δ u , {\displaystyle u_{t}=-{\mathcal {D}}{\frac {\delta H}{\delta u}},\,} with Hamiltonian H = ∫ − ∞ + ∞ ( 1 2 u 2 + 1 6 u 3 ) d x {\displaystyle H=\int _{-\infty }^{+\infty }\left({\tfrac {1}{2}}u^{2}+{\tfrac {1}{6}}u^{3}\right)\,{\text{d}}x\,} and operator D = ( 1 − ∂ x 2 ) − 1 ∂ x . {\displaystyle {\mathcal {D}}=\left(1-\partial _{x}^{2}\right)^{-1}\,\partial _{x}.}

Here δ H / δ u {\displaystyle \delta H/\delta u} is the variation of the Hamiltonian H ( u ) {\displaystyle H(u)} with respect to u ( x ) , {\displaystyle u(x),} and ∂ x {\displaystyle \partial _{x}} denotes the partial differential operator with respect to x . {\displaystyle x.}

Conservation laws

[edit]

The BBM equation possesses exactly three independent and non-trivial conservation laws.[3] First u {\displaystyle u} is replaced by u = − v − 1 {\displaystyle u=-v-1} in the BBM equation, leading to the equivalent equation:

v t − v x x t = v v x . {\displaystyle v_{t}-v_{xxt}=v\,v_{x}.}

The three conservation laws then are:[3]

v t − ( v x t + 1 2 v 2 ) x = 0 , ( 1 2 v 2 + 1 2 v x 2 ) t − ( v v x t + 1 3 v 3 ) x = 0 , ( 1 3 v 3 ) t + ( v t 2 − v x t 2 − v 2 v x t − 1 4 v 4 ) x = 0. {\displaystyle {\begin{aligned}v_{t}-\left(v_{xt}+{\tfrac {1}{2}}v^{2}\right)_{x}&=0,\\\left({\tfrac {1}{2}}v^{2}+{\tfrac {1}{2}}v_{x}^{2}\right)_{t}-\left(v\,v_{xt}+{\tfrac {1}{3}}v^{3}\right)_{x}&=0,\\\left({\tfrac {1}{3}}v^{3}\right)_{t}+\left(v_{t}^{2}-v_{xt}^{2}-v^{2}\,v_{xt}-{\tfrac {1}{4}}v^{4}\right)_{x}&=0.\end{aligned}}}

Which can easily expressed in terms of u {\displaystyle u} by using v = − u − 1. {\displaystyle v=-u-1.}

Linear dispersion

[edit]

The linearized version of the BBM equation is:

u t + u x − u x x t = 0. {\displaystyle u_{t}+u_{x}-u_{xxt}=0.}

Periodic progressive wave solutions are of the form:

u = a e i ( k x − ω t ) , {\displaystyle u=a\,\mathrm {e} ^{i(kx-\omega t)},}

with k {\displaystyle k} the wavenumber and ω {\displaystyle \omega } the angular frequency. The dispersion relation of the linearized BBM equation is[2]

ω B B M = k 1 + k 2 . {\displaystyle \omega _{\mathrm {BBM} }={\frac {k}{1+k^{2}}}.}

Similarly, for the linearized KdV equation u t + u x + u x x x = 0 {\displaystyle u_{t}+u_{x}+u_{xxx}=0} the dispersion relation is:[2]

ω K d V = k − k 3 . {\displaystyle \omega _{\mathrm {KdV} }=k-k^{3}.}

This becomes unbounded and negative for k → ∞ , {\displaystyle k\to \infty ,} and the same applies to the phase velocity ω K d V / k {\displaystyle \omega _{\mathrm {KdV} }/k} and group velocity d ω K d V / d k . {\displaystyle \mathrm {d} \omega _{\mathrm {KdV} }/\mathrm {d} k.} Consequently, the KdV equation gives waves travelling in the negative x {\displaystyle x} -direction for high wavenumbers (short wavelengths). This is in contrast with its purpose as an approximation for uni-directional waves propagating in the positive x {\displaystyle x} -direction.[2]

The strong growth of frequency ω K d V {\displaystyle \omega _{\mathrm {KdV} }} and phase speed with wavenumber k {\displaystyle k} posed problems in the numerical solution of the KdV equation, while the BBM equation does not have these shortcomings.[2]
